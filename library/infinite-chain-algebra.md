# Infinite Chain Algebra

## Abstract

This paper introduces infinite chain algebra as a first step toward the notion
of analytic distance between operations. A pair of operations is interpreted as
a segment in the logical space of all possible binary operators. The analytic
arc that connects addition to multiplication is the logarithm, since
multiplication is addition transported through logarithmic coordinates. From
this point of view, ordinary arithmetic is only one local algebra determined by
one analytic distance. The same idea can be extended into an infinite sequence
of binary operations

$$
\oplus_1,\oplus_2,\oplus_3,\oplus_4,\ldots
$$

where every consecutive pair

$$
(\oplus_n,\oplus_{n+1})
$$

forms a closed local algebra on a suitable domain. The first two operations are
ordinary addition and multiplication. The third operation is not ordinary
exponentiation and not tetration. It is instead the commutative logarithmic
power

$$
a\oplus_3 b=a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}=\exp(\log a\log b).
$$

The whole chain is generated by repeatedly lifting the previous operation
through the logarithm and the exponential. The same mechanism can also be
inverted, allowing us to search for the predecessor of addition. More generally,
changing the analytic arc changes the distance and can generate exotic algebras.

## Motivation

The ordinary pair

$$
(+,\cdot)
$$

already contains a structural relation:

$$
ab=\exp(\log a+\log b).
$$

Thus multiplication can be viewed as addition transported through the logarithm.
This suggests a different extension of arithmetic from the hyperoperation
hierarchy. Instead of passing from multiplication to ordinary exponentiation
$a^b$, we pass to the operation obtained by transporting multiplication through
logarithmic coordinates:

$$
\exp(\log a\log b).
$$

This operation is naturally commutative:

$$
a^{\log b}=\exp(\log a\log b)=\exp(\log b\log a)=b^{\log a}.
$$

The goal is to continue this construction indefinitely.

The deeper goal is to use this construction as a way to measure the separation
between operations. Addition and multiplication are not simply two unrelated
rules; they are two points connected by a specific analytic transformation. The
logarithm is the arc that makes the passage possible.

## Analytic Distance Between Operators

Let $F$ and $G$ be two binary operations. We say that $G$ is analytically
adjacent to $F$ if there exists an invertible analytic map $\phi$ such that

$$
G(a,b)=\phi^{-1}(F(\phi(a),\phi(b))).
$$

The map $\phi$ acts as an analytic arc from $F$ to $G$. It transports the
operation $F$ into a new coordinate system and then brings the result back to
the original space.

In this sense, the pair $(F,G)$ determines a segment in the space of operators:

$$
F \xrightarrow{\phi} G.
$$

The analytic distance between $F$ and $G$ is not a numerical length at first. It
is the type of analytic deformation required to transform one operation into the
other. If $\phi$ is the identity, then no distance is traveled. If $\phi$ is the
logarithm, then the segment is logarithmic.

For addition and multiplication we have

$$
ab=\exp(\log a+\log b).
$$

Thus multiplication is the logarithmic transport of addition:

$$
\cdot=\mathcal{L}(+),
$$

where

$$
\mathcal{L}F(a,b)=\exp(F(\log a,\log b)).
$$

The ordinary arithmetic algebra $(+,\cdot)$ is therefore the local algebra
created by the logarithmic distance.

## The Logarithmic Lift

Let $F$ be a binary operation defined on a positive domain. Its logarithmic lift
is the operation $\mathcal{L}F$ defined by

$$
(\mathcal{L}F)(a,b)=\exp(F(\log a,\log b)).
$$

This transforms an operation acting on logarithmic coordinates into an operation
acting on the original variables.

The infinite chain is defined recursively by

$$
\oplus_{n+1}=\mathcal{L}\oplus_n.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
a\oplus_{n+1} b=\exp((\log a)\oplus_n(\log b)).
$$

The first operation is addition:

$$
a\oplus_1 b=a+b.
$$

Then every later operation is obtained from the previous one by this logarithmic
lift.

## The Predecessor of Addition

The logarithmic lift gives the forward rule

$$
\oplus_{n+1}=\mathcal{L}\oplus_n.
$$

It is natural to ask whether the chain can be extended backward. In particular,
we ask for an operation $\oplus_0$ such that

$$
\mathcal{L}\oplus_0=\oplus_1,
$$

where

$$
\oplus_1=+.
$$

By definition of the logarithmic lift, this means

$$
a+b=\exp((\log a)\oplus_0(\log b)).
$$

Taking the logarithm of both sides gives

$$
(\log a)\oplus_0(\log b)=\log(a+b).
$$

Now set

$$
x=\log a, \qquad y=\log b.
$$

Then

$$
a=e^x, \qquad b=e^y,
$$

and the predecessor operation is

$$
x\oplus_0 y=\log(e^x+e^y).
$$

Thus the predecessor of addition is the operation

$$
\boxed{x\oplus_0 y=\log(e^x+e^y)}.
$$

This operation is known analytically as the log-sum-exp operation. In this
framework it has a structural meaning: it is the operation whose logarithmic
lift is ordinary addition.

Indeed,

$$
\exp((\log a)\oplus_0(\log b))
=\exp(\log(e^{\log a}+e^{\log b}))
=a+b.
$$

Therefore

$$
\mathcal{L}\oplus_0=+.
$$

The visible arithmetic chain can now be extended one step to the left:

$$
\oplus_0 \longrightarrow + \longrightarrow \cdot
\longrightarrow (a,b\mapsto a^{\log b}) \longrightarrow \cdots.
$$

Addition is no longer the absolute beginning of the structure. It is the
logarithmic lift of a softer operation, log-sum-exp.

The predecessor has its own identity element. Since

$$
\log(e^x+e^{-\infty})=x,
$$

the neutral element of $\oplus_0$ is $-\infty$ in the extended real line. This
matches the idea that the predecessor of ordinary addition lives naturally on a
completed logarithmic domain.

The operation is also commutative and associative, because it is transported
from ordinary addition through the exponential coordinate:

$$
x\oplus_0 y=\log(e^x+e^y).
$$

Thus $\oplus_0$ forms a closed local algebra with $+$ on the extended real
domain where $-\infty$ is allowed.

More generally, if $\mathcal{L}\oplus_{n-1}=\oplus_n$, then the inverse rule is

$$
x\oplus_{n-1}y=\log(e^x\oplus_n e^y).
$$

This gives a way to continue the chain to the left and study operations before
addition.

## First Operators in the Chain

Starting with

$$
\oplus_1=+,
$$

we obtain

$$
a\oplus_2 b
=\exp(\log a+\log b)
=ab.
$$

Thus the second operation is multiplication.

Applying the same rule again,

$$
a\oplus_3 b
=\exp((\log a)(\log b)).
$$

Therefore

$$
a\oplus_3 b=a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}.
$$

This is the first new operation of the chain. It is commutative, unlike ordinary
exponentiation.

The next operation is

$$
a\oplus_4 b
=\exp((\log a)\oplus_3(\log b)).
$$

Since

$$
u\oplus_3 v=\exp(\log u\log v),
$$

we get, for $a,b>1$,

$$
a\oplus_4 b
=\exp\left(\exp(\log(\log a)\log(\log b))\right).
$$

Equivalently,

$$
a\oplus_4 b
=\exp\left((\log a)^{\log(\log b)}\right)
=\exp\left((\log b)^{\log(\log a)}\right).
$$

The fifth operation is then

$$
a\oplus_5 b
=\exp((\log a)\oplus_4(\log b)).
$$

Expanding once gives, on the domain where all logarithms are defined,

$$
a\oplus_5 b
=\exp\left(
\exp\left(
\exp(\log(\log(\log a))\log(\log(\log b)))
\right)
\right).
$$

Thus each new operation adds another logarithmic layer inside and another
exponential layer outside.

## General Form

Define the iterated logarithm by

$$
\log^{[0]}x=x,
$$

and

$$
\log^{[k+1]}x=\log(\log^{[k]}x).
$$

Then the first operations suggest the pattern

$$
a\oplus_n b
=\exp^{[n-1]}\left(\log^{[n-1]}a+\log^{[n-1]}b\right),
$$

where $\exp^{[m]}$ denotes $m$ repeated exponentials.

For $n=1$, this gives

$$
a\oplus_1 b=a+b.
$$

For $n=2$, it gives

$$
a\oplus_2 b=\exp(\log a+\log b)=ab.
$$

For $n=3$, it gives

$$
a\oplus_3 b
=\exp\left(\exp(\log(\log a)+\log(\log b))\right).
$$

This expression simplifies to

$$
\exp(\log a\log b),
$$

which is exactly

$$
a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}.
$$

Thus the third operation is not tetration: it is multiplication inside
logarithmic coordinates.

## Closure of Consecutive Pairs

Because each operation is produced by a logarithmic lift, the natural domain
shrinks as the chain rises. The pair

$$
(\oplus_1,\oplus_2)=(+,\cdot)
$$

is closed on positive real numbers:

$$
a+b>0, \qquad ab>0.
$$

The pair

$$
(\oplus_2,\oplus_3)
$$

is also closed on positive real numbers:

$$
ab>0,
$$

and

$$
a\oplus_3 b=\exp(\log a\log b)>0.
$$

The pair

$$
(\oplus_3,\oplus_4)
$$

requires $a,b>1$, because $\oplus_4$ contains $\log(\log a)$ and
$\log(\log b)$. Higher pairs require higher iterated logarithms to be defined.
Thus each adjacent pair has its own natural domain.

For every $n$, once the domain is chosen so that all logarithms involved are
defined, the pair

$$
(\oplus_n,\oplus_{n+1})
$$

is closed by construction, because the outer exponential returns a positive
value.

## Local Algebraic Layers

The local algebras are the consecutive windows

$$
A_n=(S_n,\oplus_n,\oplus_{n+1}),
$$

where $S_n$ is a domain on which both operations are defined and closed.

The first layer is

$$
A_1=(S_1,+,\cdot).
$$

The second layer is

$$
A_2=(S_2,\cdot,\star),
$$

where

$$
a\star b=a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}.
$$

The third layer is

$$
A_3=(S_3,\star,\oplus_4).
$$

Each operation is the upper operation of one layer and the lower operation of
the next. This overlap is what makes the structure a chain.

## Classifying Distances

The logarithmic lift is only one possible analytic distance. If $\phi$ is any
invertible analytic function on a suitable domain, we may define the
$\phi$-lift of an operation $F$ by

$$
(\mathcal{L}_{\phi}F)(a,b)=\phi^{-1}(F(\phi(a),\phi(b))).
$$

Different choices of $\phi$ generate different algebras.

For example, with

$$
\phi(x)=\log x,
$$

we recover multiplication from addition:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\log}(+)(a,b)=ab.
$$

With

$$
\phi(x)=x^p,
$$

on a positive domain, the induced operation is

$$
\mathcal{L}_{x^p}(+)(a,b)=(a^p+b^p)^{1/p}.
$$

For $p=1$ this is ordinary addition. For $p=2$ it becomes

$$
(a^2+b^2)^{1/2},
$$

which resembles a Euclidean norm. Thus changing the analytic arc changes the
local algebra.

With

$$
\phi(x)=\frac{1}{x},
$$

one obtains

$$
\mathcal{L}_{1/x}(+)(a,b)=\frac{ab}{a+b},
$$

on domains where the expression is defined. This is another exotic operation:
it is not ordinary addition or multiplication, but it is generated by analytic
transport from addition.

This suggests a classification program:

1. Choose a class of analytic arcs $\phi$.
2. Generate the transported operation $\mathcal{L}_{\phi}F$.
3. Study the local algebra $(F,\mathcal{L}_{\phi}F)$.
4. Compare different arcs by the algebraic laws they preserve or destroy.

In this framework, ordinary arithmetic is only the logarithmic case. Other
choices of analytic distance may generate exotic algebras with different
notions of closure, identity, inverse, and distributivity.

## Exotic Chains

Once a distance operator $\mathcal{L}_{\phi}$ is fixed, one may iterate it:

$$
\oplus_{n+1}=\mathcal{L}_{\phi}\oplus_n.
$$

The logarithmic chain studied above corresponds to $\phi=\log$. But a different
choice of $\phi$ produces a different infinite chain:

$$
+\longrightarrow \mathcal{L}_{\phi}(+)\longrightarrow
\mathcal{L}_{\phi}^2(+)\longrightarrow\cdots.
$$

Therefore the main object is not only a single chain, but the space of all
chains generated by analytic distances. Two chains may be compared by asking
whether their arcs are equivalent under a change of coordinates, whether their
local algebras are isomorphic, and which algebraic laws remain invariant along
the chain.

## Examples

For $a=2$ and $b=3$, the first operations are

$$
2\oplus_1 3=5,
$$

$$
2\oplus_2 3=6,
$$

and

$$
2\oplus_3 3=2^{\log 3}=3^{\log 2}=\exp(\log 2\log 3).
$$

Numerically,

$$
2\oplus_3 3\approx 2.140695.
$$

This value is not $2^3=8$ and is not tetration. It is the commutative logarithmic
power generated by lifting multiplication.

For $\oplus_4$, the values $a=2$ and $b=3$ are not in the simplest real domain
for the expanded formula, because it involves $\log(\log 2)<0$. On domains where
higher iterated logarithms are positive, for example sufficiently large $a$ and
$b$, the next operators are real and closed.

## Structural Principle

The guiding principle is:

> Each operation is obtained from the previous one by moving into logarithmic
> coordinates, applying the previous operation, and returning by the exponential.

Symbolically,

$$
\oplus_{n+1}=\mathcal{L}\oplus_n.
$$

Thus multiplication is the logarithmic lift of addition, and the commutative
operation

$$
a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}
$$

is the logarithmic lift of multiplication.

## Conclusion

Infinite chain algebra is an extension of the algebra generated by addition and
multiplication, but its deeper purpose is to introduce analytic distance between
operations. The ordinary arithmetic pair $(+,\cdot)$ is the local algebra formed
by the logarithmic distance. It is not the only possible algebra, and it is not
the usual hyperoperation hierarchy. In the logarithmic case the visible arithmetic chain is

$$
+ \longrightarrow \cdot \longrightarrow (a,b\mapsto a^{\log b})
\longrightarrow \oplus_4 \longrightarrow \oplus_5 \longrightarrow \cdots.
$$

But the same construction also reveals a predecessor of addition:

$$
x\oplus_0 y=\log(e^x+e^y).
$$

Therefore a longer chain is

$$
\oplus_0 \longrightarrow + \longrightarrow \cdot
\longrightarrow (a,b\mapsto a^{\log b}) \longrightarrow \cdots.
$$

The third operation is commutative because

$$
a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}.
$$

All later operations are generated recursively by

$$
a\oplus_{n+1}b=\exp((\log a)\oplus_n(\log b)).
$$

The theory studies the infinite sequence of local algebras formed by consecutive
pairs. Each pair is closed on its natural analytic domain, and the passage from
one layer to the next is controlled by the chosen analytic lift.

The broader problem is classification: determine which analytic distances exist,
which algebras they generate, and which laws survive along their chains. In this
sense, conventional arithmetic is one distinguished point in a larger landscape
of possible exotic algebras.
