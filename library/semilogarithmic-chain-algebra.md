# Semilogarithmic Chain Algebra

## Abstract

This paper combines two constructions: infinite chain algebra and the
semilogarithm. Infinite chain algebra studies operations connected by analytic
arcs, such as the logarithmic arc that sends addition to multiplication. The
semilogarithm is a function $s$ satisfying

$$
s(s(x))=\log x.
$$

If the logarithm represents one unit of analytic distance, then the
semilogarithm represents half of that distance. This allows us to define an
operator algebra whose consecutive steps are half-steps between the usual
logarithmic layers. The result is a refined chain of operations inserted between
addition, multiplication, and the higher logarithmic operations.

## Background

In infinite chain algebra, the logarithmic lift of a binary operation $F$ is

$$
(\mathcal{L}_{\log}F)(a,b)=\exp(F(\log a,\log b)).
$$

Starting with addition,

$$
a\oplus_1 b=a+b,
$$

the logarithmic lift gives multiplication:

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\log}(+)(a,b)=ab.
$$

Applying the same lift again gives the commutative logarithmic power

$$
a\oplus_3 b=\exp(\log a\log b)=a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}.
$$

This creates the logarithmic chain

$$
+\longrightarrow \cdot\longrightarrow (a,b\mapsto a^{\log b})
\longrightarrow \cdots.
$$

The semilogarithm introduces a finer scale. If

$$
s\circ s=\log,
$$

then $s$ behaves as a half-logarithm under composition.

## The Semilogarithmic Lift

Assume that $s$ is an invertible semilogarithm on a suitable domain. Define the
semilogarithmic lift of a binary operation $F$ by

$$
(\mathcal{L}_sF)(a,b)=s^{-1}(F(s(a),s(b))).
$$

This is the same analytic transport principle used for the logarithmic lift, but
with $s$ replacing $\log$.

Since

$$
s\circ s=\log,
$$

we also have

$$
s^{-1}\circ s^{-1}=\exp,
$$

where the inverse is understood on the chosen branch. Therefore applying the
semilogarithmic lift twice gives

$$
\mathcal{L}_s^2=\mathcal{L}_{\log}.
$$

Indeed,

$$
\mathcal{L}_s(\mathcal{L}_sF)(a,b)
=s^{-1}\left((\mathcal{L}_sF)(s(a),s(b))\right),
$$

and

$$
(\mathcal{L}_sF)(s(a),s(b))
=s^{-1}(F(s(s(a)),s(s(b)))).
$$

Since $s(s(x))=\log x$, this becomes

$$
\mathcal{L}_s^2F(a,b)
=s^{-2}(F(\log a,\log b)).
$$

But $s^{-2}=\exp$, so

$$
\mathcal{L}_s^2F(a,b)=\exp(F(\log a,\log b))
=\mathcal{L}_{\log}F(a,b).
$$

Thus the semilogarithmic lift is a square root of the logarithmic lift.

## Half-Step Operations

Let

$$
\odot_0=+.
$$

Define the semilogarithmic chain by

$$
\odot_{k+1}=\mathcal{L}_s\odot_k.
$$

Then

$$
\odot_1(a,b)=s^{-1}(s(a)+s(b)).
$$

This operation is the half-step between addition and multiplication. It is not
ordinary multiplication, but applying the same lift once more gives

$$
\odot_2=\mathcal{L}_s^2(+)=\mathcal{L}_{\log}(+)=\cdot.
$$

Therefore

$$
+\longrightarrow \odot_1\longrightarrow \cdot
$$

is a refined segment in the space of operations.

Continuing,

$$
\odot_3=\mathcal{L}_s(\cdot),
$$

so

$$
\odot_3(a,b)=s^{-1}(s(a)s(b)).
$$

This is the half-step between multiplication and the logarithmic power. Applying
another semilogarithmic lift gives

$$
\odot_4=\mathcal{L}_s^2(\cdot)=\mathcal{L}_{\log}(\cdot).
$$

Hence

$$
\odot_4(a,b)=\exp(\log a\log b)=a^{\log b}=b^{\log a}.
$$

The refined chain begins as

$$
+\longrightarrow s^{-1}(s(a)+s(b))\longrightarrow \cdot
\longrightarrow s^{-1}(s(a)s(b))
\longrightarrow (a,b\mapsto a^{\log b})\longrightarrow\cdots.
$$

The usual logarithmic chain is recovered by taking every second operation:

$$
\odot_{2n}=\oplus_{n+1}.
$$

## General Form

The logarithmic chain is generated by the lift

$$
\mathcal{L}_{\log}.
$$

The semilogarithmic chain is generated by

$$
\mathcal{L}_s,
$$

with

$$
\mathcal{L}_s^2=\mathcal{L}_{\log}.
$$

Thus semilogarithmic chain algebra refines analytic distance by allowing
half-integer levels. If the logarithmic chain is indexed by integer distance,
then the semilogarithmic chain is indexed by half-distance:

$$
0,\frac{1}{2},1,\frac{3}{2},2,\ldots
$$

The operation at distance $n/2$ from addition is

$$
\odot_n=\mathcal{L}_s^n(+).
$$

Even levels reproduce the logarithmic chain:

$$
\odot_{2m}=\mathcal{L}_{\log}^m(+),
$$

while odd levels are new intermediate operations:

$$
\odot_{2m+1}=\mathcal{L}_s(\mathcal{L}_{\log}^m(+)).
$$

This is the operator analogue of placing a semilogarithm between the identity
and the logarithm.

## Effect of a Normalization

The semilogarithm is not unique without additional normalization. If one chooses

$$
s\left(\frac{1}{2}\right)=0,
$$

then the half-step operation

$$
\odot_1(a,b)=s^{-1}(s(a)+s(b))
$$

is tied to the value chain

$$
e\mapsto \sqrt e\mapsto 1\mapsto \frac{1}{2}\mapsto 0
\mapsto -\log 2\mapsto -\infty.
$$

If instead one chooses

$$
s\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt e}\right)=0,
$$

then the corresponding chain becomes

$$
e\mapsto e^{1/\sqrt e}\mapsto 1\mapsto \frac{1}{\sqrt e}
\mapsto 0\mapsto -\frac{1}{2}\mapsto -\infty.
$$

Thus a normalization of $s$ does not merely choose values of a function. It
selects a specific half-distance geometry in the space of operations, and
therefore selects a specific family of intermediate algebras.

## Analytic Distance Interpretation

In ordinary infinite chain algebra, addition and multiplication are separated by
one logarithmic unit:

$$
+\xrightarrow{\log}\cdot.
$$

With the semilogarithm, this unit is split into two equal analytic steps:

$$
+\xrightarrow{s}\odot_1\xrightarrow{s}\cdot.
$$

Likewise, multiplication and logarithmic power are split as

$$
\cdot\xrightarrow{s}\odot_3\xrightarrow{s}(a,b\mapsto a^{\log b}).
$$

The semilogarithm therefore creates a new class of exotic algebras located at
half-integer positions between the classical logarithmic layers.

This suggests a broader classification problem. If $r$ is a fractional iterate
of the logarithm satisfying

$$
r^{\circ q}=\log,
$$

then the lift $\mathcal{L}_r$ would generate $q$ equally spaced intermediate
operator layers between each logarithmic layer. The semilogarithm is the case
$q=2$.

## Conclusion

Semilogarithmic chain algebra inserts a half-step structure into infinite chain
algebra. The key identity is

$$
\mathcal{L}_s^2=\mathcal{L}_{\log},
$$

which follows from

$$
s\circ s=\log.
$$

Starting from addition, the semilogarithmic lift produces the intermediate
operation

$$
\odot_1(a,b)=s^{-1}(s(a)+s(b)),
$$

and applying the lift again recovers multiplication:

$$
\odot_2(a,b)=ab.
$$

Thus the semilogarithm does not only interpolate values of a function. It also
interpolates algebraic operations. It creates a refined operator chain in which
ordinary arithmetic appears as the even-indexed part of a larger half-distance
algebra.
