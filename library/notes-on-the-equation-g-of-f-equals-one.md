# Notes on the Equation g(f(x)) = 1

*Preliminary notes on degenerate compositions, and on when a constant can only
emerge in the limit of infinite iteration.*

## Abstract

We study the functional equation

$$
g(f(x)) = 1 \qquad \text{for all } x \in \mathbb{R},
$$

asking for two non-constant functions whose composition is the constant $1$. We
show that continuous non-constant solutions exist and are completely understood
— they require $g$ to be flat on the image of $f$ — but that the analytic
category collapses: no two non-constant real analytic functions satisfy the
equation. We then reframe the problem dynamically, replacing exact composition
by the asymptotic statement $\lim_{n\to\infty} g(f^{(n)}(x)) = 1$, which escapes
the analytic rigidity and connects the question to the theory of infinite
applications.

## 1. The Problem

We seek

$$
f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}
$$

such that

$$
g(f(x)) = 1
$$

for every $x$, while excluding the trivial solutions in which $f$ or $g$ is
globally constant.

## 2. First Observations

If the composition $g \circ f$ is constant, then every value produced by $f$
must lie in the level set

$$
g^{-1}(1) = \{\, y \in \mathbb{R} : g(y) = 1 \,\}.
$$

Equivalently,

$$
\operatorname{Im}(f) \subseteq g^{-1}(1).
$$

The image of $f$ must lie entirely inside the set where $g$ equals one. The
problem is thus not about $f$ and $g$ separately, but about the geometric
relation between the *range* of $f$ and a *single level set* of $g$.

## 3. The Continuous Case

Assume $f$ and $g$ continuous, $f$ non-constant, and $g(f(x)) = 1$.

Since $f$ is continuous on the connected domain $\mathbb{R}$, its image is an
interval. Therefore $g$ must equal $1$ on an entire interval.

**Example.** Let

$$
f(x) = \tanh(x), \qquad \operatorname{Im}(f) = (-1, 1).
$$

Any continuous $g$ with $g(y) = 1$ for all $y \in (-1,1)$ — and arbitrary
behaviour outside — yields $g(f(x)) = 1$. Continuous non-constant solutions
therefore exist in abundance.

## 4. Piecewise Constructions

Explicit solutions are built by giving $g$ a flat plateau. With
$f(x) = \tanh(x)$ and

$$
g(y) =
\begin{cases}
1 & |y| \le 1, \\[4pt]
1 + (|y| - 1)^{2} & |y| > 1,
\end{cases}
$$

one has $g(f(x)) = 1$ identically. The construction is honest but ad hoc: it
works precisely because $g$ is allowed a region of pure flatness.

## 5. The Analytic Collapse

Strengthen the hypotheses: let $f$ and $g$ be **analytic**, both non-constant,
with $g(f(x)) = 1$.

Since $f$ is non-constant and continuous, its image contains an interval. On
that interval $g \equiv 1$. By the **Identity Theorem** for real analytic
functions, a function equal to $1$ on a set with an accumulation point must
equal $1$ everywhere. Hence $g \equiv 1$, contradicting the assumption that $g$
is non-constant.

> **Theorem.** There do not exist two non-constant real analytic functions $f,
> g$ with $g(f(x)) = 1$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$. The only analytic solution
> is $g \equiv 1$.

The flexibility of the continuous case — the freedom to be flat on one interval
and not another — is exactly what analyticity forbids. A plateau, for an
analytic function, propagates to the whole line.

## 6. Geometric Interpretation

Read $f$ as a projection onto a subset $S = \operatorname{Im}(f)$, and $g$ as a
map that assigns the value $1$ to every point of $S$. Then

$$
g(f(x))
$$

cannot distinguish among different values of $f(x)$: all the information
generated by $f$ is annihilated by $g$. The composition is a *measurement that
returns the same reading no matter what it measures*.

## 7. A Degenerate Fixed-Point System

The equation may be seen as a degenerate dynamical step. Where a fixed-point
system aims to *preserve* information, the chain

$$
x \;\mapsto\; f(x) \;\mapsto\; g(f(x))
$$

always terminates at the same value. The two-step dynamics collapses the entire
line onto a single point — the limiting case of the attractor classes in
[[the-elementary-theory-of-the-infinite-application-of-a-function]].

## 8. From One Step to Infinite Iteration

The one-step collapse $g(f(x)) = 1$ sits beside the iterative question of when
repeated application

$$
f^{(n)}(x) = \underbrace{f(f(\cdots f(x) \cdots))}_{n}
$$

settles or oscillates. For instance $f(x) = -x$ generates the period-two orbit

$$
1, -1, 1, -1, \ldots
$$

which never collapses to a point. Exact composition forces an immediate
verdict; iteration lets the verdict be deferred — possibly forever.

## 9. The Asymptotic Reformulation

The rigidity of §5 is an artefact of demanding *exact* equality. A richer
problem replaces it by an asymptotic one:

$$
\lim_{n \to \infty} g\bigl(f^{(n)}(x)\bigr) = 1.
$$

Here $f$ and $g$ may both remain non-trivial — even analytic — and the value
$1$ appears only as an **asymptotic attractor** rather than an identity. This is
no longer a problem of composition but of dynamics: $g$ reads off a conserved
quantity that the orbit of $f$ approaches but need not reach.

**Example.** If $f^{(n)}(x) \to L$ for all $x$ and $g$ is any continuous
function with $g(L) = 1$, then $g(f^{(n)}(x)) \to 1$ with no flatness required
of $g$ anywhere. The level-set constraint of §2 is relaxed to a single point.

## 10. Onion Functions and the Composed Derivative

The asymptotic reformulation of §9 places the problem squarely inside the theory
of **onion functions** of
[[the-elementary-theory-of-the-infinite-application-of-a-function]].

**Premise.** That theory advances the hypothesis that the familiar functions are
not primitive: each is the *collapse* — the stable residue — of an iterative
process. A named function $F$ is only the outer layer; beneath it lie hidden
iterative strata, like the skins of an onion, so that

$$
F = \mathrm{GOL}^{\infty}
$$

for some unknown generator $\mathrm{GOL}$. The **direct problem** computes a
fate, $f \to f^{\infty}$; the **inverse (archaeological) problem** recovers the
generator behind an observed $F$.

The equation $g(f(x)) = 1$ is exactly an instance of the inverse problem. We
observe a flat output — the constant $1$ — and ask which inner layers $f$ and
$g$ could have produced it. The continuous solutions of §3–§4 say that *some*
onion produces the plateau; the analytic theorem of §5 says that *no analytic
onion* does, unless $g$ was the trivial layer $g \equiv 1$ to begin with.

**The composed derivative.** Differentiating the constraint by the chain rule
turns the static equation into a relation between layers:

$$
\frac{d}{dx}\, g(f(x)) = g'\!\bigl(f(x)\bigr)\, f'(x) = 0 .
$$

This *composed derivative* must vanish identically. Since $f$ is non-constant,
$f'(x) \neq 0$ on a dense set, forcing

$$
g'\!\bigl(f(x)\bigr) = 0 \quad\text{on } \operatorname{Im}(f).
$$

So $g'$ vanishes on the entire image of $f$ — the plateau of §4 reappears, this
time *derived* rather than assumed, and the analytic collapse of §5 becomes the
statement that a derivative vanishing on a set with an accumulation point must
vanish everywhere.

**Why this is the useful object.** Reading $g \circ f$ as an onion, the composed
derivative is the tool that peels it: where iteration $f^{\infty}$ follows the
horizontal *trajectory*, the operator

$$
D^{\infty}(F) = F \to F' \to F'' \to \cdots
$$

strips the vertical *structure*, one layer per differentiation. The product
$g'(f)\,f'$ is precisely the first peel of the composite — it records how each
inner layer contributes to the outer flatness. A profitable program is therefore
to iterate it: study the higher composed derivatives

$$
\frac{d^{k}}{dx^{k}}\, g(f(x))
$$

as the successive skins of the constant $1$, and ask which generators
$\mathrm{GOL}$ leave $1$ invariant under both operators at once — a fixed point
of iteration that is also flat under infinite differentiation. Such an object
would be, to the composed derivative, what the exponential is to $D$ and the
Dottie constant is to $\cos$.

## 11. Conclusions

1. Continuous non-constant solutions of $g(f(x)) = 1$ exist.
2. They require $g$ to be constant ($=1$) on the image of $f$.
3. If $f$ is continuous and non-constant, that image contains an interval.
4. Hence $g$ must equal $1$ on a whole interval.
5. If $g$ is analytic, the Identity Theorem forces $g \equiv 1$.
6. Consequently there are no non-trivial analytic solutions.
7. Richer behaviour requires abandoning analyticity, restricting domains,
   allowing piecewise constructions, or moving from exact composition to
   infinite iteration and asymptotic limits.

## 12. Open Questions

- Can a meaningful notion of *infinite composition derivative* be defined?
- Can the constant $1$ emerge only in the limit of repeated composition, never
  exactly?
- Can oscillatory systems with attractor set $\{-1, 1\}$ play the role of
  *generalized constants* under a suitable averaging of $g$?
- Is there an algebra of infinite compositions analogous to differentiation and
  integration?
